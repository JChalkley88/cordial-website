If you have tried using ChatGPT or Claude to write a customer email, a newsletter, or a quarterly update, you will know the problem. The output sounds like a press release written by an American intern. It uses "excited" four times. It opens with "I hope this finds you well." It signs off with "looking forward to hearing from you" whether you wanted it to or not.

The default voice of these tools is the first thing most owners hit, and it is the reason a lot of them give up after a fortnight. The voice is breezy, padded, and faintly transatlantic. It does not sound like a British small business and it does not sound like the person whose name is at the bottom of the email.

The good news is you can fix it, and the fix is more durable than most people think. What you want is a tone of voice agent: a saved setup that produces drafts sounding like your business, not like everyone else's.

Here is how to set one up properly, in either Claude or ChatGPT.

## Start with samples, not adjectives

The instinct is to describe your voice. "Professional but friendly. Confident. Approachable." This is useless. Every business on earth claims those four things, and the model has read all of them. The output regresses to the average.

What works is examples. Go and find five or six pieces of writing that actually sound like you. A good email to a difficult client. A proposal that won the work. A note you wrote when you were slightly annoyed. Paste the full text in. Real sentences carry information that adjectives cannot. The model picks up that you never use exclamation marks, that you open with the point rather than the throat-clearing, that you say "happy to" rather than "delighted to."

If you only do one thing from this note, do this. Six real samples beat a page of description.

## Setting it up in Claude

Claude has a feature called Projects. Create one and call it something obvious like "Customer Writing." In the project's custom instructions, paste your samples and then add a short brief. A good one reads roughly like this: "Write in the style of the examples below. British English. No exclamation marks. No em-dashes. Get to the point in the first sentence. Never use 'excited', 'delighted', or 'reach out'. If you are unsure of a fact, ask rather than invent it."

The banned-words list matters more than you would guess. Pick the three or four words you would never say and forbid them by name. It is crude and it works.

The advantage of a Project is that everything you do inside it inherits the voice. You are not re-pasting instructions every time. You open the project, say "draft a reply to this," and it already knows.

## Setting it up in ChatGPT

ChatGPT has two routes. The quick one is Custom Instructions, found under your account settings. There is a box asking how you want responses. Put your brief and a couple of samples there. The catch is that this applies to everything you do, including the unrelated stuff, so it can get in the way.

The better route, if you have a paid account, is a custom GPT. Click your name, then "My GPTs," then create. You give it a name, paste the same samples and brief into the instructions, and you can upload files too. A document of ten past emails gives it more to draw on. Once saved, it sits in your sidebar like a separate tool. Worth building two if you write in genuinely different registers, for instance one for sales replies and one for internal updates.

## The bit everyone skips

A tone agent is not finished when you build it. The first drafts will be 80 per cent right and 20 per cent off. The work is in correcting the 20 per cent and feeding it back.

When a draft uses a phrase you hate, do not just delete it. Tell the agent. "Stop using 'in order to', say 'to'." "You keep starting paragraphs with 'Additionally'. Don't." In Claude you add these to the project instructions. In a custom GPT you edit the configuration. After a fortnight of small corrections, the drafts get to the point where you barely touch them.

Keep the corrections specific. "Be more natural" does nothing. "Cut any sentence that starts with 'I am writing to'" does plenty.

## What it is actually for

The point is not to remove yourself from your own writing. It is to skip the blank page.

A quarterly customer update that used to take most of a Sunday afternoon can, with a properly trained agent, take closer to fifteen minutes. The agent does the dull 70 per cent. You do the 30 per cent that needs a human who knows the customers, reading every line, changing a few, sending it.

That is the right division of labour. Build it once, correct it for a couple of weeks, and it will quietly save you an hour or two a week for years. Not bad for a Sunday afternoon's setup, done properly this time.

If you want a second pair of eyes on which parts of your writing it would be most useful to automate first, that is part of what an audit covers. Either way, I write something like this most weeks. You are welcome to follow along.
